## Quick facts
- Cocoricó is a small roasted chicken delivery restaurant owned by me (André)
- At Rua General Roca 91, Tijuca, Rio de Janeiro - Brazil
- Has been running for 2 years
- For the past year it operated on the red, with a few profitable months on the first year
- Requires around 1000 to 1500 of external investment (from me) every month to stay afloat
- Has a stable ~12k BRL of monthly operating income
- Has around 40k BRL of CAPEX
- Opens only on weekends, Friday, Saturday and Monday, and serves from 10:30am to 3:00pm
- Has a loose but long term team of around 8 people
- Has nominal capacity of 80 orders in 4 hours (from 11am to 15pm) and  peak capacity of 40 orders in 1h (from 11:30 to 12:30)
- Requires 4 people to run operations on a normal day, and 5 to run at peak capacity
- Current operational roles are: cook & heat operator; packager & cleaner; cashier; motoboy
- Currently, the cook & heat operator is a critical resource without replacement
- Has an average demand of 4-12 orders on Friday, 18-24 on Saturday, and 36-60 on Mondays
- Has a stable and recurring customer base
- Has good online scores and above average product
- Priced bellow competition
- Has zero investment on marketing; has a regional influencer (500mi followers) that wants to partner
- For the last year my father, which lives nearby, replaced me on the more operational work and as all-arounder support which currently is necessary

## The Original (and Current) Motivation
- Build a self-financed small business end-to-end, as a track record for acquiring investment for other business ideas, since self-financing is limited and unsustainable long term, has I have to work on one side as engineer, to put money on the other
- The gastronomy scene in Rio is terrible, restaurants can't cook a simple steak properly. There is an obvious quality gap that is easy to address, without incurring significant operational costs, and there lies the opportunity.
	- Most cooking practices are dogmatic and cooks are usually retarded - they don't think from first principles and just do the same thing they've been doing for the past 100 years (e.g., overcooking chicken, seasoning as if spices come from India on sailboats and are expensive)
	- And most investors in that realm don't care about good food either, they invest more on the "experience", wood work and overpriced equipment then on the actual dishes - which partially reflects the local culture.

## The Operating Philosophy (Short Version)
- 90% of restaurants suck, and 99% of roasted chicken products suck even more. I've experimented a dozen before opening the restaurant.
- To an extent, a good product doesn't need marketing.
- Consistency (low variation) is more important than having the best product
- But still, the goal is to have a great product. The short mantra is to have **consistently superior product** than that of local competitors.
- I rather have a small dedicated long-term and well-paid team than to have high turnover and lacking consistency. I don't personally need the profit from this store, and the idea is to turn 100% of it to the team as bonuses (but I need it to stop leaking).
- People are adults and don't need managers on top of them if the right incentives are in place - this is a belief that I had, weaker by the day, but to an extent an existential premisse of the business, since I knew from day 1 the store wouldn't be able to afford a dedicated manager.
